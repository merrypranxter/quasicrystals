# Discovery Timeline: Quasicrystals

## Overview

The discovery of quasicrystals represents one of the most dramatic scientific controversies of the 20th century — a story of paradigm-shattering observation, fierce resistance, ultimate vindication, and Nobel recognition.

---

## 1974 — Penrose Tiles

**Roger Penrose** (Oxford) discovers a set of two tiles that can cover the plane aperiodically (without ever repeating). The two shapes — later called "kite" and "dart" — produce a 5-fold symmetric pattern that is impossible in conventional crystallography.

Penrose tiling is initially treated as a mathematical curiosity, a puzzle toy. No one suspects it describes real matter.

---

## 1976–1981 — Mathematical Foundations

**Robert Ammann** independently discovers multiple aperiodic tile sets, including what becomes the Ammann-Beenker tiling (8-fold symmetry). He also discovers Ammann bars — lines running through Penrose tilings that form a quasiperiodic pattern.

**Nicolaas de Bruijn** (1981) publishes the "multigrid method" — proving that Penrose tilings can be generated by projecting a 5D cubic lattice onto 2D at an irrational angle. This is the origin of the cut-and-project method.

---

## 1982 — Shechtman's Electron Diffraction

**April 8, 1982**: **Dan Shechtman** at the National Bureau of Standards (now NIST) is analyzing a rapidly solidified Al-Mn alloy using electron diffraction. His notebook entry reads: *"10 fold???"*

The diffraction pattern shows **sharp Bragg peaks** — indicating long-range order — but with **10-fold (decagonal) symmetry**. By the rules of conventional crystallography, 5-fold and 10-fold symmetry are mathematically impossible in any periodic crystal.

Shechtman's colleagues and supervisors initially dismiss the result. He is asked to leave his research group. His supervisor hands him a textbook on crystallography.

---

## 1984 — Publication and Shockwave

After two years of verification and searching for alternate explanations, Shechtman and colleagues (Blech, Gratias, Cahn) publish:

> **"Metallic Phase with Long-Range Orientational Order and No Translational Symmetry"**  
> *Physical Review Letters*, November 12, 1984

The crystallography community erupts. Two-time Nobel laureate **Linus Pauling** becomes the most prominent skeptic, insisting the result must be "icosahedral twinning" — a known crystal artifact producing apparent 5-fold symmetry from multiple intergrown crystals.

Pauling reportedly says: *"There is no such thing as quasicrystals, only quasi-scientists."*

---

## 1985 — Theoretical Framework

**Dov Levine and Paul Steinhardt** (Penn) publish a theoretical model explaining Shechtman's results as a **3D quasicrystal** — a physical analog of the Penrose tiling in three dimensions. They introduce the term "quasicrystal."

They show that the icosahedral symmetry in 3D is mathematically analogous to the 5-fold symmetry in 2D Penrose tilings, and that the cut-and-project method from a 6D hypercubic lattice produces the observed diffraction patterns.

---

## 1987 — Stable Quasicrystals

Early quasicrystals were metastable — they would transform to conventional crystals when annealed. Critics argued they were merely poorly-ordered "quasi-periodic" structures.

**1987**: Al-Li-Cu icosahedral quasicrystal synthesized — the first **thermodynamically stable** quasicrystal. This removes the argument that quasicrystals are just metastable accidents.

**1987 (independently)**: Al-Pd-Mn quasicrystal grown as near-perfect single grains with sharp diffraction peaks — comparable to the best single crystals. Pauling's twinning hypothesis is definitively ruled out.

---

## 1990s — Establishment and Expansion

Hundreds of quasicrystalline compositions discovered across multiple alloy systems:
- Zn-Mg-RE (rare earth) icosahedral quasicrystals
- Al-Co-Ni, Al-Ni-Co decagonal quasicrystals  
- Cd-Yb icosahedral quasicrystal (2000)

International Union of Crystallography (IUCr) revises the definition of "crystal" in **1992** to include aperiodic crystals, explicitly acknowledging quasicrystals.

---

## 2000s — Soft-Matter and Biological Quasicrystals

**2004**: Quasicrystalline ordering discovered in soft matter — block copolymers and liquid crystals self-assembling into 12-fold quasiperiodic patterns.

**2009**: **Luca Bindi** et al. discover the first **natural quasicrystal** — icosahedrite (Al₆₃Cu₂₄Fe₁₃) — in a meteorite sample from the Khatyrka River, Chukotka, Russia. The meteorite is traced to the CV3 carbonaceous chondrite class. The sample dates to the early solar system (~4.5 billion years ago), making it the oldest known quasicrystal.

---

## 2011 — Nobel Prize in Chemistry

**Dan Shechtman** is awarded the **Nobel Prize in Chemistry** for the discovery of quasicrystals.

The Nobel Committee explicitly references the initial controversy: "The discovery of quasicrystals fundamentally altered how chemists conceive of solid matter... The discovery was met by disbelief. 'Impossible!' said the two-time Nobel laureate Linus Pauling."

---

## 2015 — Second Natural Quasicrystal

Bindi et al. discover a **second natural quasicrystal** in the same Khatyrka meteorite: a decagonal Al₇₁Ni₂₄Fe₅ phase with 10-fold symmetry.

The meteorite is shown to have undergone a high-velocity collision in space — the shock event may have created conditions favorable for quasicrystal formation.

---

## 2019–Present — Frontiers

- Quasicrystals discovered in **nuclear bomb test sites** (Trinity site soil) — shock conditions analogous to the meteorite collision.
- 2D quasicrystalline order found in **graphene** and **moiré superlattices** — a new class of quasicrystal at the atomic scale.
- Photonic quasicrystals — structures mimicking quasicrystal geometry in optical materials — produce unusual light propagation properties.
- Topological properties of quasicrystals attract condensed matter physics interest.
